## **Commercial Decision Logic & Guardrails**

### **(How Amin Actually Decides, Approves, and Blocks)**

---

## **1\. Purpose of This Document**

This document captures **how Amin makes commercial decisions in reality**.

It translates:

* instinct

* experience

* pattern recognition

* risk judgement

into **explicit logic and guardrails** that the Commercial App must later enforce.

This document exists to prevent:

* automating the wrong behaviour

* turning judgement into noise

* scaling mistakes

---

## **2\. How Amin Decides (Reality, Not Theory)**

Across the transcripts, Amin does **not** decide based on a single metric.

He balances **five decision dimensions simultaneously**:

1. Margin and profitability

2. Customer quality

3. Operational feasibility

4. Strategic fit

5. Timing and context

Any system that ignores one of these will fail.

---

## **3\. Core Decision Principle**

**Volume is never more important than sustainability.**

Amin consistently prioritizes:

* controlled growth

* predictable margin

* long-term customer value

Over:

* short-term volume

* aggressive discounting

* operational heroics

This principle governs all downstream logic.

---

## **4\. Margin Guardrails (Implicit Rules)**

### **How Amin thinks**

* Every deal has a minimum acceptable margin.

* Margin acceptability changes based on:

  * utilization

  * customer quality

  * strategic importance

* Discounts are acceptable only within controlled limits.

### **Guardrails to extract**

* A deal below minimum margin is **not automatically rejected**, but:

  * it must trigger explicit approval

  * the reason must be recorded

* Repeated low-margin deals are a warning sign.

### **Why this matters**

Without margin guardrails:

* pricing becomes emotional

* negotiation power erodes

* profitability is discovered too late

---

## **5\. Customer Quality Logic (ICP vs ECR)**

### **How Amin thinks**

Amin differentiates between:

* **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)**: who the company wants

* **Existing Customer Reality (ECR)**: who the company actually has

He intuitively evaluates:

* payment behaviour

* operational complexity

* relationship effort

* growth potential

* cultural fit

### **Guardrails to extract**

* Not all revenue is good revenue.

* Some customers must be:

  * priced defensively

  * constrained contractually

  * or exited over time

* New customers must be screened against future strategy, not past habits.

---

## **6\. Operational Feasibility Logic**

### **How Amin thinks**

Before approving a deal, he asks internally:

* Can we actually deliver this consistently?

* Are we promising speed or flexibility that will break ops?

* Will this customer create daily exceptions?

### **Guardrails to extract**

* Deals that require operational heroics must be flagged.

* SLAs must reflect **reality**, not aspiration.

* If feasibility is unclear, approval must pause.

---

## **7\. Strategic Fit Logic**

### **How Amin thinks**

Amin evaluates deals in context:

* Does this customer align with where we want to be in 2–5 years?

* Does it strengthen or dilute our position?

* Does it distract from core lanes, sectors, or regions?

### **Guardrails to extract**

* Some deals are acceptable today but not repeatable.

* Strategy overrides short-term pressure.

* The system must remember strategy even when people forget.

---

## **8\. Timing and Context Logic**

### **How Amin thinks**

The same deal is judged differently depending on:

* current utilization

* pipeline health

* budget performance

* market conditions

### **Guardrails to extract**

* Aggressive pricing may be acceptable when utilization is low.

* Defensive pricing is required when capacity is tight.

* Context must be visible at decision time.

---

## **9\. Proposal Approval Logic**

### **How Amin thinks**

A proposal is approved when:

* pricing risk is understood

* assumptions are explicit

* exclusions are clear

* no hidden promises exist

### **Guardrails to extract**

* Proposals must not rely on free-text promises.

* Risk must be visible before approval.

* Exceptions must be deliberate, not accidental.

---

## **10\. SLA Gating Logic (Critical)**

### **How Amin thinks**

* SLA is **not** a sales tool.

* SLA is a delivery protection mechanism.

* SLA should only be defined once commercial intent is confirmed.

### **Guardrails to extract**

* SLA creation must be blocked until commercial approval.

* SLA cannot change pricing or scope.

* SLA must expose penalties and exclusions clearly.

---

## **11\. Escalation Logic**

### **How Amin thinks**

* Not every issue needs escalation.

* Only issues that threaten:

  * margin

  * customer relationship

  * strategic position  
     should reach him.

### **Guardrails to extract**

* The system must filter noise.

* Amin should see exceptions, not everything.

* Escalation thresholds must be explicit.

---

## **12\. What Happens When Guardrails Are Missing**

From the transcripts:

* Amin becomes the bottleneck.

* Decisions slow down.

* Risk increases.

* Knowledge does not scale.

This confirms that **guardrails are more important than automation**.

# **DOCUMENT 4**

## **Quote, Proposal & SLA Flow Requirements**

### **(How Commercial Work Must Actually Happen)**

---

## **1\. Purpose of This Document**

This document defines the **correct commercial flow** for:

* quoting

* proposing

* negotiating

* approving

* formalizing delivery commitments

It exists to ensure the Commercial App:

* matches how deals are actually done

* does not slow negotiations

* protects margin and delivery

* removes Amin as a bottleneck

This document is **non-negotiable doctrine** for system design.

---

## **2\. Core Commercial Principle**

**Deals are won through quotes and proposals.**  
 **Risk is controlled through SLAs.**

These are different stages with different purposes and must never be conflated.

---

## **3\. Required Commercial Flow (Authoritative)**

The system must enforce the following sequence:

**Opportunity → Quote → Proposal → Negotiation → Commercial Approval → SLA → Contract → Live**

Key rule:  
 **SLA is not required to quote or propose.**

---

## **4\. Quote Stage Requirements**

### **What a Quote Is**

A quote is:

* a commercial pricing position

* based on assumptions

* designed to open negotiation

### **What a Quote Must Include**

* customer and opportunity reference

* service scope (warehouse, transport, bundled, VAS)

* volume assumptions

* pricing line items

* total expected revenue

* internal cost assumptions

* margin visibility

* quote validity period

* explicit assumptions and exclusions

### **What the System Must Enforce**

* minimum margin visibility

* discount thresholds

* mandatory assumptions

* mandatory exclusions

* version control

### **What the Quote Must NOT Include**

* SLA commitments

* binding service guarantees

* operational promises beyond scope

**Outcome of Quote Stage**  
 A commercially defensible price position that can move forward quickly.

---

## **5\. Proposal Stage Requirements**

### **What a Proposal Is**

A proposal is:

* a structured commercial narrative

* built from an approved quote

* designed for negotiation and positioning

### **Proposal Can Be Created With**

* quote only

* quote \+ high-level service description

* quote \+ **indicative (non-binding) service language**

### **Mandatory Proposal Safeguard**

If no SLA exists, the proposal must clearly state:

“Service levels are indicative and subject to final SLA agreement.”

This must be **system-enforced**, not optional.

### **What the System Must Enforce**

* proposal cannot change approved quote pricing

* proposal must lock quote version

* proposal versions tracked during negotiation

* risk flags visible internally

### **What the Proposal Must NOT Do**

* introduce binding SLA terms

* promise penalties or guarantees

* override exclusions

**Outcome of Proposal Stage**  
 A negotiable, safe commercial document that protects the business.

---

## **6\. Negotiation Stage Requirements**

### **What Happens Here**

* customer challenges pricing, scope, assumptions

* internal revisions may be required

* multiple proposal versions may exist

### **What the System Must Do**

* track proposal versions

* record why changes were made

* preserve audit trail

* keep margin and risk visible

### **What the System Must Prevent**

* silent scope creep

* pricing changes without re-approval

* loss of context across versions

**Outcome of Negotiation Stage**  
 Clear commercial intent and agreed direction.

---

## **7\. Commercial Approval Gate (Critical Control)**

### **What Commercial Approval Means**

Commercial approval confirms:

* pricing is accepted internally

* margin is understood and approved

* scope is agreed

* customer is accepted

* strategic fit is confirmed

### **System Enforcement**

* SLA creation is **blocked** until this status is reached

* approval must be explicit

* approvals must be role-based

* reasons for exceptions must be captured

This gate is the **most important control point** in the entire system.

---

## **8\. SLA Stage Requirements (Post-Approval Only)**

### **What an SLA Is**

An SLA is:

* a delivery protection mechanism

* an operational agreement

* a risk control instrument

It is **not** a sales tool.

### **When SLA Can Be Created**

Only when:

* commercial approval is complete

* customer intent is confirmed

### **What the SLA Must Include**

* KPIs and definitions

* measurement method

* reporting frequency

* penalties and credits

* exclusions and caps

* customer responsibilities

* escalation paths

### **What the System Must Enforce**

* SLA references the approved quote and proposal version

* SLA cannot change pricing or scope

* SLA requires operational feasibility sign-off

* SLA versioning and approval tracking

**Outcome of SLA Stage**  
 A delivery-safe, enforceable agreement.

---

## **9\. Contract Readiness Rules**

### **What the System Must Enforce**

* contract cannot be marked “ready to sign” without:

  * approved quote

  * approved proposal

  * approved SLA

* all documents must reference the same version lineage

This prevents legal and operational risk.

---

## **10\. Failure Modes This Flow Prevents**

This structure explicitly prevents:

* deals slowing due to premature SLA work

* margin leakage through hidden promises

* operational overload

* Amin reviewing every proposal

* disputes about “what was agreed”

---

This document establishes that:

* quotes and proposals are tools to win deals

* SLAs are tools to protect delivery and margin

* the system must enforce timing and separation

* commercial speed and operational safety are not opposites
